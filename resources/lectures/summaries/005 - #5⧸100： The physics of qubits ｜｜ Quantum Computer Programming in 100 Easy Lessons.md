# The physics of qubits

- Large everyday objects almost never show visible superposition, so practical qubits are usually microscopic systems such as photons and electrons.
- Two concrete qubit realizations are introduced: photon polarization (horizontal vs. vertical) and electron spin (down vs. up).
- Measurement devices make the abstract states observable: a polarizing filter blocks or transmits photons, and a Stern-Gerlach device sends electrons to one of two detectors.
- If a qubit is in a superposition `x|0> + y|1>`, measurement gives outcome `0` with probability `x^2` and outcome `1` with probability `y^2`.
- Key takeaway: amplitudes are not what we directly observe; measurement converts them into ordinary random outcomes whose probabilities come from squaring the amplitudes.
